# Debris Flow Volume Validation

## Data Sources

| Source | Description |
|--------|-------------|
| **Observed** | 227 field-measured debris flow volumes from USGS post-fire surveys |
| **Predicted** | TORRENT per-watershed volumes using Gartner et al. (2014) ln(V) model |
| **Storm scenario** | 10-year return period, 1-hour duration (NOAA Atlas 14) |

### Key References

- Gartner, J.E. et al. (2014). Empirical models for predicting volumes of sediment deposited by debris flows and sediment-laden floods in the transverse ranges of southern California. *Engineering Geology*, 176, 45-56.
- Cannon, S.H. et al. (2010). Storm rainfall conditions for floods and debris flows from recently burned areas in southwestern Colorado and southern California. *Geomorphology*, 96, 250-269.
- Santi, P.M. et al. (2008). Sources of debris flow material in burned areas. *Geomorphology*, 96, 310-321.

## Methodology

1. Each USGS observation has a deposit location (lat/lon) and measured volume.
2. Each TORRENT sub-watershed has a pour point and predicted volume.
3. Observations are matched to the nearest TORRENT sub-watershed within 5.0 km.
4. Metrics are computed on log-transformed volumes (standard practice for debris flow volume validation, as volumes span 4+ orders of magnitude).

## Caveats

1. **Storm mismatch**: TORRENT uses a 10-year design storm (NOAA Atlas 14). USGS volumes were measured after actual storms with varying intensities. This is the largest source of expected disagreement.
2. **Spatial matching**: Pour-point to deposit-point matching introduces uncertainty. Some USGS observations may map to adjacent sub-watersheds.
3. **Temporal mismatch**: TORRENT models immediate post-fire conditions. Some USGS observations occurred 1-3 years post-fire with partial vegetation recovery.
4. **Volume definition**: TORRENT predicts initiation-zone volume. USGS measures deposit volume, which may include bulking (entrainment) or debulking (deposition) along the flow path.
