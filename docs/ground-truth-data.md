## 2. Schultz Fire (2010) — Flagstaff, Arizona

### Event Summary
- **Fire:** Schultz Fire, June 2010
- **Area burned:** 6,100 ha (15,070 acres)
- **Burn severity:** 55% high severity
- **Location:** San Francisco Peaks, near Flagstaff, Arizona
- **Post-fire fatality:** 1 (flooding)
- **Total cost (10-year):** $95.8-$100.7 million (2021 dollars, excluding property value changes)
- **Initial 3-year cost estimate:** $133-$147 million

### Observed Debris Flow Data

| Parameter | Value | Source |
|---|---|---|
| Basins producing debris flows (July 20 event) | 19 of 30 sub-basins on upper slopes | Youberg et al. 2011 |
| Additional basins (August 16 event) | 23 basins produced debris flows | Youberg et al. |
| Total basins assessed | 48 (morphometric, soils, burn severity data) | Youberg / USGS |
| Channel incision depth | up to 4 m in former swales | Youberg et al. 2011 |
| Flood magnitude increase | 1-2 orders of magnitude above pre-fire | Youberg et al. 2011 |
| Sheet flow depth (residential areas) | 0.3-0.6 m (1-2 ft); deeper in washes | NWS Flagstaff |
| Number of post-fire flooding events (2010 monsoon) | 16 total (1 major + 15 lesser) | NWS Flagstaff |
| Homes flooded (July 20 event) | ~85 total (45 + 40 in two areas) | NWS Flagstaff |
| Homes at future high risk | up to 300 | NWS Flagstaff |

### Triggering Rainfall

| Parameter | Value | Source |
|---|---|---|
| July 20 peak rainfall | 45 mm in 45 minutes | Youberg et al. 2011 |
| July 20 peak 10-min intensity | 24 mm / 10 min | Youberg et al. 2011 |
| July 20 total rainfall on burn scar | 25-75 mm (1-3 inches) | NWS Flagstaff |
| Monsoon context | 4th wettest on record for Flagstaff | Youberg et al. 2011 |

### USGS Model Comparison
- Morphometric, soils, and burn severity data from 48 basins were entered into USGS models with rainfall data from multiple storms
- Individual basin volumes are available in the USGS Inventory of 227 Postfire Debris-Flow Volumes (Schultz Fire entries included)
- Note: Specific per-basin volumes (reported range 3,000-15,000 m3) are in the USGS data release CSV but not published in summary form in the papers

## 3. Grizzly Creek Fire (2020) — Glenwood Canyon, Colorado

### Event Summary
- **Fire:** Grizzly Creek Fire, August 10 - December 18, 2020
- **Area burned:** 13,000 ha (32,000 acres)
- **Burn severity:** 12% high, 43% moderate, 45% low/unburned
- **Location:** Glenwood Canyon, Colorado (I-70 corridor)
- **Infrastructure cost:** >$116 million road repairs; I-70 closures cost ~$1M/hr in lost commerce

### Observed Debris Flow Data

| Parameter | Value | Source |
|---|---|---|
| Total debris flows mapped (Year 1, 2021) | 40 debris flows in 25 drainages | Wall et al. 2024 (NHESS) |
| Storms producing debris flows (2021) | 9 of 49 storms during monsoon season | Wall et al. 2024 |
| Total erosional volume (lidar-derived) | 460,000 +/- 16,700 m3 (34 channels) | Wall et al. 2024 |
| Individual basin volume range | 160 +/- 25 m3 to 107,000 +/- 3,800 m3 | Wall et al. 2024 |
| Year 2 debris flows (2022) | 0 (despite 56 storms, 8 above threshold) | Wall et al. 2024 |
| I-70 road closures (2021-2022) | 14 total | Wall et al. 2024 |
| Lidar level of detection | +/- 10 cm | Wall et al. 2024 |

### Triggering Rainfall

| Parameter | Value | Source |
|---|---|---|
| Year 1 I15 threshold (M1 model, P50) | 25.9 mm/hr | Wall et al. 2024 |
| Year 2 I15 threshold (M1 model, P75) | 33.7 mm/hr | Wall et al. 2024 |
| Landsat-modified Year 2 threshold | 40 mm/hr | Wall et al. 2024 |
| Critical event (July 22, 2021) | Peak I15 = 21.3 mm/hr (temporary Colorado River blockage) | Wall et al. 2024 |
| 2021 storm inventory | 49 storms (monsoon season) | Wall et al. 2024 |
| 2022 storm inventory | 56 storms (June 18 - Sep 17) | Wall et al. 2024 |

### USGS Model Performance

| Metric | Value | Source |
|---|---|---|
| M1 threshold capture rate (Year 1) | 8 of 9 debris-flow storms exceeded I15 threshold (89%) | Wall et al. 2024 |
| Single false negative | June 27, 2021 (attributed to incomplete gauge network) | Wall et al. 2024 |
| Volume model overestimation | Median 4.4x overestimate | Wall et al. 2024 |
| Volume model relationship | V_observed = 0.21 * V_predicted + 180 | Wall et al. 2024 |
| Year 2 model conservatism | Too conservative; no debris flows despite 8 storms above threshold | Wall et al. 2024 |
| Vegetation recovery (Year 1) | 16-34% (avg 24%) | Wall et al. 2024 |
| Vegetation recovery (Year 2) | 50-68% (avg 57%) | Wall et al. 2024 |

### Monitoring Infrastructure
- 11 rain gauges (3 USGS initial + 7 added summer 2021 + 1 CDOT)
- Max gauge-to-observation distance: 6.8 km; average 4.2 km
- Repeat lidar for volume measurements

## Data Gaps and Notes

1. **Montecito** has the most complete published dataset: volume, velocity, inundation mapping, flow depth, and building damage inventory all available as USGS data releases.

2. **Grizzly Creek** is the gold standard for rainfall threshold validation due to the dense rain gauge network and 2-year monitoring period with repeat lidar.

3. **Schultz Fire** per-basin volumes exist in the USGS 227-volume inventory CSV but are not summarized in accessible publications. The Youberg et al. field data and AZGS geodatabase contain the detailed per-drainage measurements.

4. **Cameron Peak** has the most dramatic human impact (4 fatalities from a ~1-year return period storm), making it critical for demonstrating why low-threshold warnings matter. Specific volumes are in the USGS inventory but not in summary publications.

5. **East Troublesome** is the weakest validation fire -- limited to BAER qualitative assessments and news reports. It may be better suited as a burn-severity prediction validation target (using the USGS predicted dNBR dataset) rather than a debris flow volume/velocity validation target.

6. **USGS 227-Volume Inventory** (Wall et al. 2023): The downloadable CSV at https://www.usgs.gov/data/inventory-227-postfire-debris-flow-volumes-34-fires-western-united-states contains per-basin volumes for both Schultz and Cameron Peak fires. This should be downloaded and parsed for exact per-basin numbers.

7. **Volume model bias:** The Grizzly Creek study found the USGS volume model overestimates by 4.4x median. This systematic bias should be accounted for in TORRENT validation comparisons.

8. **Rainfall threshold model (M1):** Performs well in Year 1 (89% capture at Grizzly Creek) but becomes too conservative in Year 2 due to vegetation recovery and sediment depletion.
